Design Process

From the beginning of this project, I knew that I wanted to work with a crime-related dataset and present the findings using a choropleth map. However, I was initially torn between two datasets that both stood out to me: a Los Angeles crime dataset covering 2020 to the present, and a national FBI Supplementary Homicide Reports dataset spanning 1980 to 2014. After examining both options more closely, I ultimately chose the national dataset because it offered far greater analytical depth. It included over 35 years of data, covered all 50 states plus Washington, D.C., and contained multiple categorical dimensions such as weapon type, victim demographics, perpetrator relationships, and case clearance status.

In contrast, while the Los Angeles dataset provided strong geographic detail, it was limited to a single city and a much shorter time frame, which restricted its overall storytelling potential. Additionally, although I have approximately 2.5 years of experience designing emails and websites using HTML and some JavaScript, I have never designed a choropleth map before. Because of this, working with a nationwide dataset felt more intuitive, as visualizing data across the entire United States is both easier to implement at a high level and more visually compelling than focusing on a single city or state.

For the design of the visualization itself, my initial vision for the choropleth map was fairly by the book. The map would display the United States, include a color scale at the bottom, and feature a dropdown menu that allows users to switch between different metrics. Where I began to incorporate more of my own ideas was in making the choropleth map interactive. First, I wanted to include a sidebar that would initially appear blank, but once an individual state is clicked, it would populate with detailed metric information for that specific state. I also wanted to include an additional sidebar that highlights the top 10 states for each selected metric. While I briefly considered also displaying the bottom 10 states, I ultimately decided against it, as it could make the visualization feel cluttered and harder to interpret.

Design Choices

When it comes to my design choices, the decision I struggled with the most was the color scale. It essentially became a balance between what I personally preferred and what I believed might be the most effective design choice, and in the end, preference won. As seen in the final visualization, I chose to use a much bolder color scale, starting at black for the lowest values and transitioning to a bright color for the highest values.

I preferred this approach over a smoother, single-hue gradient that shifts from light to dark. I found the multi-color scale to be more visually dramatic and eye-catching, allowing states with higher metric values to stand out more clearly. This makes it easier for viewers to quickly identify the top-performing states. For many of the metrics included, there are a small number of states that stand out significantly compared to the rest, and this design choice emphasizes those differences effectively.

That said, this approach does come with a tradeoff. It can make it more difficult to compare states that have similar values. However, I addressed this limitation by incorporating interactive tooltips, which display the exact value for each state when hovered over. This allows users to still make precise comparisons when needed, while maintaining the strong visual impact of the color scale.

Another design choice I included in order to make the visualization more engaging was the addition of a play button for the Total Homicides (by Year) metric. I actually got this idea from one of my fellow cohort’s visualization critiques, where incorporating a video helped viewers more clearly understand the progression of time. Initially, I had only included a slider at the bottom that allowed users to manually select a specific year. However, I realized I could elevate the visualization by adding a play button that functions almost like a “video,” automatically updating the choropleth map over time, and convey the passage of time in a more natural way.

I also thought it would be valuable to include a ranking system within the sidebar to allow viewers to see how their selected state compares to others. From the visualization critiques, I remember commenting on several examples that lacked this feature and instead required viewers to manually count or estimate rankings, which made comparisons less intuitive. At the same time, I didn’t want to include a full ranking of all states directly in the sidebar, as that would have made the visualization feel cluttered and overwhelming. Instead, I designed the feature so that the total ranking is shown only for the selected state. This approach allows users to quickly access meaningful comparative information without overloading the interface with unnecessary detail.

Another important design choice I had to make was whether or not to remove Washington, D.C. from visualization. The U.S. Atlas TopoJSON file I used did include D.C., but due to its small geographic size, it was effectively hidden on the choropleth map. Initially, I considered removing it altogether, since the common workaround is to display D.C. as a small inset box in the corner of the visualization. However, after exploring the data further, I found that D.C. had the lowest case solve rate by a wide margin. Because of this, I decided it was important to keep it included, as excluding it would have removed a meaningful and noteworthy data point.

I think the last notable design choice I made was the inclusion of both light and dark modes. Honestly, this decision was largely based on personal preference. When I first designed the visualization, I used a dark background because I tend to prefer darker tones. However, as I continued refining the design, I realized it would be beneficial to also include a light mode with a white background, since adding both options allows the visualization to better align with a wider range of user preferences and viewing conditions.

Discovery

When designing my visualization, I found the dataset to be extremely valuable, as it contained a large amount of detailed and interesting information. Because of this, I aimed to capture as much insight as possible while still ensuring that the selected metrics aligned well with a choropleth map. Among the different metrics presented in the final visualization, I was most interested in identifying which states had the highest total homicide and case solve rates.

For the total homicide’s metric, one of the key learnings is that the top four states remain consistent across all 35 years of data: California, Texas, New York, and Florida, with California holding the number one position every year. California does not just lead, it often records nearly double the number of homicides compared to Texas, which consistently ranks second. However, these are also the four most populous states in the country, which raises an important question, are these states actually more dangerous, or are the higher counts simply a reflection of their larger populations? This highlights a limitation of using total counts alone, as they do not account for differences in population size and may overrepresent larger states.

All four states reached their peak homicide counts between 1991 and 1993, with California reaching over 4,300 homicides in 1993. This spike aligns with the national crack cocaine epidemic during that time period. The fact that all four states experienced similar rises and subsequent declines suggests that this trend was driven more by a nationwide factor rather than individual state-level policies. However, it is impossible to know for sure as the information is not present in the dataset.

When examining case solve rates, what stood out most was not the states at the top, but those at the bottom. D.C. has the lowest solve rate of any entry in the dataset at 34.3%, while the second lowest, New York, sits at 54.1%. That nearly 20 percentage point gap between last and second-to-last is striking on its own, but it becomes even more remarkable when you consider that this is the nation's capital. The city that houses the federal government, the FBI headquarters, and some of the most well-funded law enforcement agencies in the country solves fewer than 1 in 3 homicide cases.
